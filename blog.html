The Industry

24/12/2017

by Cameron Barnes

If there’s one thing that I was always fairly certain of, it’s that I would’ve likely ended up working in the digital space. I didn’t always know where or how I’d get there, but ask anyone who knew me throughout the past 5 or 6 years of sitting in front of computers, and you’d be told that it was inevitable. It was obvious to me that digital was growing at a crazy rate, even though I had no indicator as to the actual scale. Looking at the Tech Nation report and the figures now, you'll note that even Theresa May's on board.

My technical skills were always an asset to me. Asking when I was 14 or 15, and I would’ve told you that I wanted to be a game developer. Minecraft — the industry-defining indie game developed by Mojang — was on the up and presented this great, developer-friendly sandbox environment. I couldn’t imagine building something like that from the ground up, but being so open, it was a great platform to learn through modding.

I wanted to be more creative and lost the heart for programming along the way. Once more, if I look back on that, it was a shame. I went to college studying IT and chose a graphics & media route, as opposed to a programming one. We dabbled in C for a bit, and though I found it fairly easy to pick up having understood the fundamentals of Java (even if I’d forgotten exactly how to write it at this point), I longed for something a little more fulfilling.

The indecision there meant that I didn’t consider university too much, or even understand entirely how it worked. I found a Digital Media/Marketing course browsing through UCAS one day, and though I still can’t put my finger on why, decided that it would be the only choice. With offers from all universities that I applied to, I chose MMU.

Perhaps inevitable given the impulse in the decision, I didn’t really ever feel it for marketing. I reasonably well in it, given the lack of real interest. I was part of groups that made this advertisement for Swizzels’ Love Hearts brand, and created this theoretical brand extension for Sony PlayStation (be warned, though it does NOT download automatically, that links to a big .PDF file). We managed solid firsts for both of those through extensive research and well-thought presentation.

However, I did decide to switch my graduation route to Web Development in my final year. I had a thing for the coding aspect of websites ever since I started, and the front-end allows me to squeeze in that itch for creativity. It took a while and a lot of back and forth, but I think that I’ve been dropped in the right place this time around.

This was perhaps the context that I was missing when previously learning these programming skills. I didn't have what I would've considered to be a 'real' reason to be extending my Java knowledge, but I've now seen something that I can apply what I learnt previously in programming to.

With the intention of progressing the web field, I’ve opted to delve into as much as I can. I love listening to podcasts, especially when doing things like commuting or getting other work done. As such, I found some relevant to the web; Syntax (though they dive a little deeper), Shop Talk, and A Responsive Web Design Podcast are my regular choices. I’ve also taken to reading on the topic - the A Book Apart ones are great, and short! I then look to attend conferences and workshops where time allows going forward. I was recently at Wordcamp Manchester 2017, and have a couple of workshops lined up.
