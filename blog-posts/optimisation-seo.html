Optimisation and SEO

24/12/2017

by Cameron Barnes

Search Engine Optimisation is what the web uses to prioritise websites in search engines like Google. Data provided when developing the website will be read by these engines, and will factor into ranking and how well the site is accepted. Websites will even sometimes be not just de-ranked, but disregarded by engines completely, if it's decided that they're making purposeful attempts to abuse the system.

SEO is also something that I’ve often heard contrasting points on as to what is and isn’t relevant - though I imagine that it’s somewhat to be expected on an ever-changing medium like the web.

Helpfully, SEO specialists moz.com produced this guide, refreshed for 2017 to include everything relevant to SEO in the current day. Line that up alongside David Dias’ front-end developer checklist, which has a short section dedicated to SEO and everything else that a developer should include, and you can build a pretty good base as to what you need.

There are a few things that stood out to me about reading these and implementing SEO into this portfolio. First is the lack of importance given to meta tags now. If three things were to be highlighted as crucial to search engine performance, you’d go for the <title>, <meta> description, and the body’s <h1> element.

Keywords, given the nature of them being easy to essentially spam and abuse, are almost entirely disregarded by major search engines like Google. I can appreciate the fact that prominence is given to meaningful, well-written titles and descriptions as opposed a series of jumbled, potentially meaningless keywords.

I’d also highlight Facebook’s OpenGraph and Twitter’s Cards. Including these within your the head of your website allow you to control how the website is presented when linked across the web.

There's an argument to be made from an analytical standpoint that while you could just leave websites to read your default titles and descriptions, it would be wise to manipulate how social sites present them. There could well be benefit to snappier, more to-the-point content given the fast, nonchalant nature of social media.

In terms of other optimisations, I’ve also made attempts to condense my media as best as I can. I’ve often neglected image size and how long it’ll take for a user to download. We naturally design for ourselves, and when we’re on fast connection, it’s often hard to notice those who aren’t.

Where my previous attempts left my background images at around 250KB, even through using Google’s Speed Insights which provides optimisation for you, this time around I’ve used Photoshop to control both the size and quality of JPEGs. I’ve made attempts to keep all images under 50KB, and where possible, keep most closer to 25KB.

In addition to that, my icons are now SVGs. The arrow on the headers and the social media links in the footers are all inline. Being SVGs makes them small downloads to any client, and inline SVG is extremely easy to manipulate as far as animation goes. Animating a JPEG or PNG background-image requires the user loading another image just to change the colour, whereas inline SVG only has to change the fill of the path in order to add a hover state colour. I've seen the light.
